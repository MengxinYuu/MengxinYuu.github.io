Background

Most international students have been through a tough time for adaptation, when emotional disorders including depression and anxiety occurs easily. However, some of them don't realize the emotional disorders lying behind their daily emotional changes. Though aware of the problems, part of them hesitate to talk to psychologist, their family and friends, which increases pressure in turns.

Moodie is a mobile app designed for international students to improve mood management and regulation by mood collecting, sharing and reflecting.

Research Process

Literature Review

We studied more than 10 papers to identify users’ theory-based motivations for mood collecting, sharing and reflecting.

User Interview

We conducted a semi-structured interview with 4 international students to learn about their requirements and frustrations.

Competitive Analysis

To Study current mood tracking apps in the US industry and learn from their advantages and disadvantages, we did a competitive analysis on 8 most famous mood tracking apps in the US industry. Three major dimensions were employed in evaluation: usage (basic features), desired features (important features), and motivation(valuable features matching with user goals).

Collecting

Mood sometimes is hard to tell and specify in words. Although some apps utilize non-verbal cues such as emojis and selfies, the method has the potential to be improved.

Sharing

The feedback users got from their sharing process is just simple response such as heart/hug icons or short sentences, which is non-empathetic.

Reflecting

Although most app help users to record their mood, it mostly for venting but not reflection. The current basic visualization of mood records can not well-support users to generate insights from their mood data, find emotion patterns and improve emotion management.

Ideation & Speed Dating

Feature #1 Identifying the primary and secondary emotions to facilitate mood collecting

Collecting both primary and secondary emotions can help people to clearly identity their mood. Collecting primary emotion can help finding the real cause behind a person’s mood, while collecting secondary emotion will help them learn about how the mood subtly changes, which is usually unconscious. Looking at both primary and secondary emotions can give a fuller picture of users’ mood patterns, which can greatly help with emotional awareness.

Feature #2 Providing non-verbal cues to facilitate mood collecting

Non-verbal cues occupy about 60-65% of human communication (Birdwhistell, 1970), which can help enhancing clarity for the messages being sent and building empathy during communication. In order to help users distinguish difference moods, the conversational bubbles we designed are coded to different colors and shapes.

Feature #3 Using conversational context to create a sense of being heard

The sense of being heard is highly valued by users. Instead of recording mood in a usual way, we designed an agent who can guide users to record their mood in a conversational context, just like chatting with a friend.


We used a storyboard to test our design idea with 12 users and got positive feedback in general: 

  • User thought recognizing moods by primary and secondary levels did help them clarify their feelings, as long as they were properly guided.
  • User enjoyed the feeling of conversation.
  • User loved the color and shape coding idea.

Besides, there are two things about documenting activities that can be improved:

  • Users were encouraged to input their activities by themselves. However, without enough guidence, they felt stuck in what to input and how to input（"Does the input format matter?"). Therefore, we added some preset options for them as a reference. Users can identify their activities by selceting existing ones or adding a new one.
  • We prompted users to document both emotional state and activities they were doing. However, current activities may not be the necessary triggers. Therefore, we added an optional notes for users to specify reasons of emotions later.


Feature #1 Anonymity sharing environment

Anonymity reinforces mood sharing. Research shows that anonymity is related to the amount of self-disclosure (McKenna & Bargh, 1999, 2000; McKenna, Green, & Gleason, 2002), which means people tend to sugarcoat themselves under their social circle of acquaintances while they feel safer to share true emotions in a relatively anonymous communication environment.

Feature #1 Non-verbal communication

To prevent uncivilized behavior or incorrect psychological guidance, Moddie doesn’t support verbal communication in this sharing process. In that context, “non-verbal” sharing means the feedback users would receive from others is non-verbal, such as doodle, drawing, emoji, etc.

Features #3 Paired sharing VS group sharing

To prevent uncivilized behavior or incorrect psychological guidance, Moddie doesn’t support verbal communication in this sharing process. In that context, “non-verbal” sharing means the feedback users would receive from others is non-verbal, such as doodle, drawing, emoji, etc.

Features #4 Doodle VS Music VS “views”

We designed three alternative sharing feedback. In a paired sharing relationship, users can receive a piece of music, or a doodle created by a replier. In a group sharing relationship, the app would inform users about times of their posts that have been viewed and "hugs" they received.


In general, users liked the ideas about non-verbal communication and anonymous sharing environment, which can make them feel safe and relaxing to share.

As for the feedback they would like to receive, 75% users preferred to receive doodle because of the personality behind the drawing:

  • Doodle is unique - it offers than one-word replies as "Sorry" or "Cong". 
  • Doodle is emphatic, and sometimes drawing communicates better than words. Users also feel more sense of "being heard" because of the effort others took in drawing. 

Moreover, users preferred the paired sharing ideas because:

  • Exposure to many posts in a group may reduce empathy and lower the amount and quality of reply in turns.
  • Paired relationship creates a close relationship and makes participants feel more responsible and willing to reply.


Data visualization about emotion changes

Our first vision is to motivate users by visualizing their achievements in collecting and reflection. For example, we have two initial ideas,  one was creating a "virtual garden" and visualizing mood records as flowers. Another one was using a "tree" as the metaphor of time and represented mood records as leaves. Moreover, the tree would grow higher with the records increasing. 

We conducted a very quick usability test to choose the ideas. Surprisingly,  the feedback from users indicated that neither of the two ideas worked. Users are willing to continually use the product because it can bring new value, therefore, intrinsic incentives are much better than extrinsic ones. This finding directed us to make more effort in creating insightful reflection experience. We decided to provide a data visualization about emotion changes, which was an explicitly benefit for users to become more aware of mood patterns and improve self-regulation

Introspection through Chatting
Users can chat with Elsa if communicating with a close friend to track their moods. Moodie enables users to identify subtle mood more easily by decomposing mood into two levels, and attribute emotion triggers accurately by recalling activity and detailed reasons.

Drawing, Instead of Saying
As the medium of reply, doodle offers more interesting, empathetic and delightful experience than words. No trolling, no cliche, doodles are surprising gifts for every user. We understand that not everyone is born as artist. Users can create doodles easily by selecting their favorite shapes and fill in color.
