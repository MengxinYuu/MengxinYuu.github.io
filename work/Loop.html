Design Challenge

Background

A special division of labor

Our client WYU (name has been changed) is one of the most successful online higher education institute in the United States and it now has more than 90,000 incoming students every year.

WYU takes a disaggregated educational model, where there is a special division of labor for the education provided to the students. There are two departments in charge of one course. Instructional Designers design all the instructions, such as the video and the textbook. Once they finished the instruction, they hand them to Instructors, who actually teach the students.

Problem

The “Shadow Curriculum” under the table

Though it’s not their role, Instructors are designing supplemental instructions for students, which is invisible to Instructional Designers and hereby is called “Shadow Curriculum”. WYU applied data-driven approach to evaluate instructional effectiveness. These “Shadow Curriculum” are created and shared outside WYU’s official instructional materials, so the effectiveness of these resources is not readily available for analysis.

Our Challenge

How might we coordinate conflicting demands of two stakeholders?

Instructional Designers and Instructors raised their requirements which were totally conflicting. Instructional Designers wished to entirely eliminate these “shadow curriculum” while Instructors were willing to do the extra.

— What they said —

Our Solution

— Problem Reframing —

Behind the conflicting attitudes about "Shadow Curriculum", we wanted to dig into the problem and looked for deeper demands of Instructors and Instructional Designers. Through the research, we reframed the problem from “What they need” to “What they want”. we found out: Instructors were seeking for the sense of inclusivity from making supplemental contents for the course they were teaching. Instructional Designers valued effectiveness so they rejected all kinds of "Shadow Curriculum" which were not available for evaluation. Lack of visibility proved to be the main reason for "Shadow Curriculum" and both sides asked for more visibility for better communication and collaboration.

— Introducing LOOP —

Contributing meaningfully by creating formative assessment

Encouraging Instructors to create in-class quizzes as a mean to use their practices to capture learning data that was formerly inaccessible.

Encouraging collaboration through feedback loop

Building a public dashboard for Instructors in the same course and making everything ready to share.

Giving and receiving information-rich feedback for better communication and collaboration.

Centralizing everything and make it visible

Storing quizzes in a centralized bank accessible for all departments to observe and evaluate.

Finding something pre-made by editing any quizzes and making it right for struggling students.

Tracking performance data to validate effectiveness

Tracking BKT, AFM and iAFM data to evaluate content efficiency and measure learning achievement at the back-end.

Supporting Instructional Designers to evaluate or freeze quizzes with problematic data at the front-end.

— What's the Outcome —

COORDINATION

Loop coordinates the conflict between Instructional Designers and Instructors by shifting Instructors’ focus from instruction creation to formative assessment creation.

VISIBILITY

Loop solves the “Shadow Curriculum” problem by centralizing the content sharing platform to offer visibility for Instructors and Instructional Designers.

INCLUSIVITY

Loop facilitates collaboration and helps Instructors feel a sustained sense of ownership of instruction alongside Instructional Designers.

EFFECTIVENESS

Loop captures learning data and helps all departments gain a better understanding of the effectiveness of course materials.

Process Overview

Exploration

— Why Instructors create "Shadow Curriculum" —

Hypothesis One

A Revolution Of Instructors’ Role

Traditionally, Instructor is used to do "everything". However, modern institutes including WYU are adopting a new disaggregated model that totally splits traditional Instructor's role into different departments, where Instructors are in charge of only the "teaching" part.

Our research shows that 82% of WYU’s instructors surveyed had been in traditional academia for more than 8 years. Our hypothesis is, though traditional responsibilities including instructional design are not encouraged, Instructors from traditional academia still carry their habits and understandings to their role at WYU, including creating their own instructions to help students.

Evidence

Qualitative And Quantitive Analysis

This hypothesis was verified through qualitative analysis of user interview and quantitive analysis of survey data.

One thing we noticed from survey data is, Instructors were willing to be involved not only into the instructional design process but also other areas such as assessment design, which is one of the triggers for us to design Loop as a formative assessment platform.

Hypothesis Two

A Disconnection Between Instructors And Instructional Designers

Instructional Designers and Instructors suppose to have the biggest impact on each other's work. However, according to WYU’s Stakeholder Map and Information Flow Model, a disconnection appearred caused by few direct connections and a long iteration cycle for feedback. The disconnection caused non-transparency for communication. Therefore, when Instructors are not clear about instructional content or find instructional content insufficient to support students, instead of communicating with Instructional Designers, they tend to make supplemental instruction (“Shadow Curriculum”) based on their expertise and understanding.

Evidence

Qualitative And Quantitive Analysis

This hypothesis was verified through qualitative analysis of user interview and quantitive analysis of survey data.

— Why "Shadow Curriculum" matters to Instructional Designers —

Reason One

Effectiveness Of "Shadow Curriculum" Is Not Available For Evaluation

Instructional Designers highly rely on data to evaluate the effectiveness of the instructions they designed, and the only data they get comes from students' assessment.

"Shadow Curriculum" provided by Instructors may have big impacts on students' assessment data, thus interfering with the evaluation of instructions designed by Instructional Designers. In addition, there is no way to evaluate the effectiveness of "Shadow Curriculum". Therefore, whether “Shadow Curriculum" really helps struggling students is impossible to be proved.

Ideation

— Brainstorming to explore more possibilities —

Based on three attributes that are Inclusivity, Visibility and Effectiveness, we organized an in-team brainstorming activity and generated 9 design ideas. We proposed a framework to show the internal relations of our design ideas from the perspectives of data we collected from WYU.

We designed scenarios and storyboard for pitching our ideas better.

— Selecting design solutions with the most potential —

Our team collaborated with WYU to rank potential solutions based on level of impact and difficulty. We chose Formative Assessment Platform as the MVP design idea because it had the highest impact on WYU's ecosystem with relatively low implement difficulty. Promising impact are expected from this idea because:
1) Formative Assessment Platform is the only one that touched all three attributes (Inclusivity, Visibility and Effectiveness) we raised.
2) WYU saw Formative Assessment idea as an opportunity to use instructors’ practices as a mean to capture learning data that was formerly inaccessible.

Iteration

— Iteration Process —

Iteration One

Test the purpose and features: Is the purpose clear to users? Does the tool add value to assessment creation? How does our solution compare to existing tools?

Iteration Two

Test the features and usability: Does the solution improve instructor processes? Would instructors give or receive peer feedback on quizzes from peers and others?

Iteration Three

Test Effectiveness (Completion of tasks / subtasks), Efficiency (Time to complete tasks / subtasks) and Satisfaction (User sentiment and feedback on process)
